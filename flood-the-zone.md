# Flood the Zone

The current show at Mark Flood's gallery isn't by Mark Flood. It's by Frisco Pete — a name Flood has been working under for some years, producing paintings that don't much resemble the lace and corporate-logo canvases that made him known. Same artist, different zone, different rules.

The practice has a long lineage. Friedrich Stowasser became Friedensreich Hundertwasser in 1949 — the name itself an act of authorship, peace-realm-hundred-water, an architecture built of syllables. The new name carried the work the old one couldn't have. Marcel Duchamp signed objects as Rrose Sélavy. Romain Gary, already a Goncourt laureate, invented Émile Ajar and won the prize a second time — a feat the Académie's rules expressly forbade, and which only became public after his death. Doris Lessing, curious whether her reputation was carrying her work, submitted two novels under the name Jane Somers; the manuscripts were turned down by her own publisher.

The reasons for slipping into another name tend to cluster. Some are financial — Stephen King's Richard Bachman existed partly because the industry would only release one King novel a year. Some are cultural — Charlotte Brontë publishing as Currer Bell, Mary Ann Evans as George Eliot. And some are simpler: the freedom of working without an audience watching you work. The known name carries expectation; the pseudonym carries permission.

The risks travel with the reasons. Gary's Ajar deception cost him — he wrote a posthumous confession before taking his own life. Lessing's Somers experiment may have revealed something she wished it hadn't. Banksy's anonymity is also a fragility — identification ends the project. There is the harder question, too, of what gets lost when the work and the name finally meet — whether the freer thing survives being claimed.

Consider Sargent in 1884. Madame X hung at the Salon under his own name, and the scandal nearly finished him in Paris; he left for London and rebuilt. One can imagine a version where the painting arrived signed otherwise — where Sargent kept the society portraits in one zone and the strange, blue-skinned experiment in another. He didn't have that option, or didn't want it.

The word *zone* is doing its own work in the present. Steve Bannon's political flood is one usage — saturation as strategy, the opposite of pseudonymous focus. Martin Amis borrowed *Interessengebiet*, the bureaucratic name for the territory around Auschwitz, and made it the title of a novel about who chooses to look. The artistic pseudonym sits somewhere between those poles: not a flood, not a selection, but a smaller act — a territory drawn around a different self, where work the main name couldn't host gets to happen.

Whether that territory holds, or whether it eventually leaks, seems to be the open question every pseudonym is asking.
